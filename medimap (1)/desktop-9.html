Rabies

Rabies is a viral disease that affects the central nervous system (CNS), caused by the rabies virus, which belongs to the Lyssavirus genus. It is primarily transmitted to humans through the bite or scratch of an infected animal, most commonly dogs. The virus is typically present in the saliva of infected animals and can enter the body through broken skin.

  Causes and Symptoms

Causes: It is primarily transmitted to humans through the bite or scratch of an infected animal, most commonly dogs. The virus is typically present in the saliva of infected animals and can enter the body through broken skin.

Once the rabies virus enters the body, it travels along peripheral nerves to the brain and spinal cord. The incubation period, the time from exposure to the onset of symptoms, can vary widely, ranging from a few days to several years, although it typically ranges from one to three months.

  1. symptoms: Prodromal stage: This stage is characterized by non-specific symptoms such as fever, headache, malaise, and discomfort at the site of the bite or scratch. There may also be tingling or numbness around the wound site. This stage usually lasts for a few days.
  2. Acute neurological stage: As the virus spreads to the central nervous system, more severe symptoms develop, including anxiety, confusion, agitation, hallucinations, hydrophobia (fear of water), difficulty swallowing, and paralysis. This stage is often fatal, with death typically occurring within a few days to weeks after the onset of symptoms, usually due to respiratory failure.
